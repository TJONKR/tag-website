
POSITIONING
For the Builders
What we say to the people who are already here, and the ones who should be.
February 2026  |  Draft for internal discussion

# The Feeling
You know that feeling at a hackathon?
Building something real. Surrounded by people who get it. The energy, the competition, the fun. You ship, you show, you maybe win. No bullshit, no meetings, no roadmaps. Just making things.
But hackathons end. Monday comes. You go back to your normal life. Back to the job you're not that excited about. Back to building alone. Back to explaining to people what you do and watching their eyes glaze over.
What if it didn't end?
That's what this is. A room in Amsterdam where the hackathon energy is permanent. Where every day feels like you're building something that matters, surrounded by people who actually give a shit. Where shipping is the norm. Where showing your work is the culture. Where the competition is friendly but real. Where someone always has the answer you're stuck on.
# The People in the Room
Some of us run companies. Some have side projects. Some are just figuring it out. Some already have revenue. Some don't have a pitch deck and probably never will. Some are technical. Some are creative. Some are both and can't tell the difference anymore.
What we have in common: we'd rather make something than talk about making something.
You'll recognize the people here. They're the ones who build on weekends because they can't stop. Who get more excited about a side project than their day job. Who text you at midnight about an idea. Who have shipped more things in a month than most teams ship in a year. Who are fun to be around because they're alive with what they're working on.
You probably don't find these people at networking events. Or at corporate innovation meetups. Or on LinkedIn. You find them in the lab at 2am. In group chats sharing what they're building. At hackathons. In places like this.
# What Actually Happens Here
## Weekdays are about momentum
People show up and work on their own thing. But you're not alone — you're surrounded by people doing the same. You get stuck, you ask the person next to you. You ship something, you show the room. You hear someone talking about a tool you've never tried, and 20 minutes later you've integrated it.
Things move fast here because everyone around you is moving fast. That's not a slogan. It's the physics of putting the right people in one room.
## Weekends are for building
The space turns into an open build room. People come to work on whatever they're obsessed with. Hackathons, build sessions, challenges. Sometimes structured, sometimes not. The only rule: you're here to make something.
## Events are for culture
Weekly community moments. Monthly hackathons. Drinks. Demos. People sharing what they've shipped, what's broken, what they learned. Not presentations — conversations. Not panels — people showing real things on real screens.
# Why This Feels Different
You've probably been to coworking spaces. You've probably sat in rooms full of people on laptops where nobody talks to each other. You've probably been to meetups where everyone's selling something. You've probably joined communities that are just Slack channels that go quiet after a week.
This isn't that.
The difference is the people. We don't accept everyone. We don't want everyone. We want the ones who build things. Who are curious and generous with what they know. Who are fun to be around. Who make the room better by being in it.
The difference is the energy. This isn't a quiet office with a coffee machine. Things are happening here. Demos, launches, experiments, failures, breakthroughs. You can feel it when you walk in.
The difference is what matters here. Not your LinkedIn title. Not your funding round. Not how many followers you have. What matters is: what are you building? What did you ship? What are you obsessed with? Show us.
# The Culture
Ship fast, learn faster. Nobody here waits until something is perfect. You ship it broken, you get feedback, you fix it, you ship again. The person who shipped three things this month is more respected than the person who's been planning one thing for six months.
Show your work. We don't talk about what we're going to build. We show what we built. Demos are the currency. Screenshots, prototypes, live walkthroughs. "Let me show you" beats "let me tell you about" every time.
Help each other. Someone's stuck on something you solved last week? You help. Someone needs an intro? You make it. Someone's idea is shit? You tell them, kindly, and then you help them make it better. The people who give the most are the most valued.
No shame, no shit. Your prototype crashes during a demo? That's fine. Your idea didn't work? That's data. You pivoted for the fifth time? At least you're moving. The only thing that's not okay is not trying.
Joy is the point. This should be the most fun you have all week. If it's not, we're doing it wrong. Ambition and joy aren't opposites — they feed each other. The best things get built by people who are having a good time building them.
# What You Actually Get
Forget the feature list for a second. Here's what people actually say changed for them after joining:
You build faster. Because the people around you are building fast. Because you can ask someone a question instead of googling for an hour. Because when you see others ship, you want to ship too.
You stop building alone. The loneliest thing about being an indie builder is having nobody who understands what you're doing. Here, everyone understands. You don't have to explain. You just show.
You get better. Because you're around people who know things you don't. Because you see how others approach problems. Because friendly competition raises your game without you even noticing.
You find your people. Co-founders, collaborators, first users, friends. The relationships that form here aren't networking — they're the side effect of building alongside people you respect.
Things happen. Projects that started as hackathon experiments become real products. Conversations at lunch turn into collaborations. Someone you met here three months ago becomes the person you build your company with.
# The Invitation
If you make things, you should be here.
We're not asking for your pitch deck. We're not asking about your runway. We're not asking where you went to school or how many years of experience you have.
We're asking one thing: are you building something?
If yes — come by. See the space. Meet the people. Feel the energy. You'll know in five minutes if this is your place.
If you're the kind of person who builds on weekends because you can't help it. If you have more ideas than hours in the day. If you're looking for people who get it. If you want to be in a room where things are actually happening.
Come to the next thing. Or just drop by.
