Good ones. Let me think about these.

AIGEN

This is interesting. It's "AI" + "gen" (generation). The AI generation. The people building with AI right now. It immediately tells you what era this community belongs to. It also sounds like "eigen" in Dutch — which means "own" / "self" / "one's own." "Eigen" is deeply Dutch: "je eigen ding doen" (doing your own thing). That's literally your philosophy.

So you've got: AI generation + Dutch "eigen" (your own thing) + it sounds like a company name that already has weight (like Vercel, Figma — short, punchy, tech-sounding). "I'm at AIGEN" works. "AIGEN Amsterdam" works. It doesn't need explaining to your target audience — builders in AI immediately get it.

The risk: it's very AI-specific. If the community evolves beyond AI (which it might), you're locked into a category. And there will be a hundred AI-something names in 2026. But if you want to own the AI builder lane in the Netherlands, it's strong.

The New Foundation

This has a completely different energy. It's institutional but rebellious — "new" says we're replacing whatever came before. "Foundation" says this is built to last, this is the base layer, this is serious infrastructure. Together it's a statement: the old foundation of the tech ecosystem isn't working, we're the new one.

It has the cultural layer positioning baked right in — a foundation is literally what everything else gets built on top of. That's your upstream thesis in one name.

"I'm at The New Foundation" — sounds important, sounds like a place. It has the same energy as "The New School" in New York — an institution that defined itself by being the alternative to the establishment.

The risk: it's long. People will shorten it to "TNF" or just "The Foundation." "The Foundation" on its own is powerful but generic — there are a lot of foundations. And the word "foundation" might confuse people into thinking you're a nonprofit or a charity.
